The Buyer Signal Framework: How to Know Which Deals Will Actually Close

Most sales teams prioritize deals the same way: whoever emailed most recently, whoever's loudest in the pipeline review, whoever the rep "has a good feeling about." None of that is signal. It's noise dressed up as intuition.

The teams that consistently forecast accurately and close on schedule aren't smarter or luckier, they're scoring three specific things and acting on the score instead of the vibe. Here's the framework, exactly as I run it with clients.

The three axes

1. Timing

Is there an actual forcing function right now, a contract renewal, a budget cycle, a triggering event (new hire, funding round, a competitor's price increase, a compliance deadline)? "They said they're interested" is not timing. A dated, external reason to act this quarter is.

2. Relevance

Does this account match the specific problem you solve best, not "fits the ICP slide," but genuinely has the exact pain your product or service is sharpest against? A logo that technically qualifies but needs your fifth-best use case will always underperform a smaller account that needs your best one.

3. Intent

What is the account actually doing, not saying? Multiple stakeholders engaging unprompted, specific technical or implementation questions, fast replies, internal introductions happening without you asking, these are behavioral signals. A single enthusiastic champion with no internal movement is not intent, it's a hope.

Scoring it

Score each axis 1 (absent) to 3 (strong). Sum the three scores, out of 9.

  • 3-4: Nurture. Keep in the pipeline, don't spend senior selling time here yet.
  • 5-6: Qualify further. Worth a targeted push to surface the missing axis, usually timing or intent.
  • 7-9: Prioritize this week. This is where your best rep's time goes, full stop.

The value isn't the math, it's that scoring forces an honest conversation. A deal a rep is emotionally attached to because it's "big" often scores a 4 the moment you ask "what's the actual timing forcing function?" out loud, and the room goes quiet.

The pattern I see constantly: teams spend equal effort on a deal that scores a 3 and a deal that scores an 8, because effort is being allocated by loudness, not signal.

Where this breaks down

Two things sink this framework in practice. First, scoring it once and never again, signal changes weekly, and a deal that was an 8 in January can quietly decay to a 4 by March if nobody's watching. Second, letting the rep who owns the deal be the only one scoring it; a second set of eyes in the pipeline review catches the deals people are attached to for the wrong reasons.

This is also exactly the kind of gap that shows up in almost every revenue audit I run, not because teams don't have data, but because nobody's forcing the data into a decision.
